Álvaro Uribe Vélez (born July 4, 1952) is a Colombian politician who served as the 32nd President of Colombia from 2002 to 2010. He is the founder and leader of the Democratic Center party, a right-wing political party, and remains one of the most influential and controversial figures in modern Colombian politics.

Uribe's presidency was defined by his Democratic Security policy, an all-out military offensive against guerrilla groups such as the FARC and ELN, funded in part by Plan Colombia, a US$2.8 billion direct foreign aid package from the United States. His administration achieved significant reductions in violence and crime during his tenure.

Early life and education

Álvaro Uribe Vélez was born on July 4, 1952, in Medellín, Colombia, to a prominent political family. He studied law at the University of Antioquia and later completed a degree in Administration at Harvard University. His father, Alberto Uribe Sierra, was a landowner and politician who was killed by the FARC in 1983, an event that profoundly shaped Uribe's political views.

Political career

Early offices

Uribe began his political career in his home department of Antioquia. He held positions in the Public Enterprises of Medellín and the Ministry of Labor, and directed the Special Administrative Unit of Civil Aeronautics (1980–1982). He became Mayor of Medellín in October 1982, served as a Senator between 1986 and 1994, and as Governor of Antioquia between 1995 and 1997.

Governor of Antioquia

As Governor of Antioquia, Uribe implemented policies that modernized the department's infrastructure and reduced local violence. His success at the departmental level built the momentum for his presidential campaign in 2002.

Presidency (2002–2010)

Democratic Security

After being elected President in 2002, Uribe launched the Democratic Security policy, a comprehensive military strategy that involved strengthening the armed forces, expanding police presence throughout the country, and conducting targeted operations against guerrilla leadership. The policy led to a dramatic reduction in kidnappings, terrorist attacks, and overall violence. Homicide rates fell significantly during his tenure.

Plan Colombia

Uribe's security policies were supported by Plan Colombia, a US$2.8 billion aid package from the United States. The funding provided military equipment, training, and intelligence support that enabled Colombian forces to regain control over territory previously dominated by guerrilla groups. Under Uribe, Colombia also saw the demobilization of the right-wing paramilitary group known as the AUC.

However, his administration was also marked by controversies, including the "false positives" scandal, in which thousands of civilians were killed by the Colombian army and reported as guerrilla combatants. Defense Minister Juan Manuel Santos, who later succeeded Uribe as president, played a role in these events.

In August 2010, Uribe was appointed vice-chairman of the UN panel investigating the Gaza flotilla raid, recognizing his international stature as a security expert.

Post-presidency

After his presidency, Uribe was elected as a Senator and founded the Democratic Center party (Centro Democrático). He remained a vocal figure in Colombian politics, often critical of the peace process with the FARC under President Juan Manuel Santos. His political influence extended through his party, which became one of the most important political forces in Colombia.

Legacy

Álvaro Uribe is a polarizing figure in Colombian and Latin American politics. Supporters credit him with transforming Colombia from a failed state into a more secure nation, while critics point to human rights abuses and authoritarian tendencies. His Democratic Security policy is studied internationally as a model for counter-insurgency. He is widely regarded as the most influential Colombian politician since the 1990s.
